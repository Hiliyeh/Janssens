Understanding the Situation

Getting locked out is one of the most stressful everyday situations. Every year in Belgium, thousands of people find themselves standing outside their home after accidentally closing their door with the keys still inside. This mishap can happen to anyone: a draft of wind, a moment of distraction, a door that closes automatically, or simply forgetting to take your keys when leaving.

The good news? In the vast majority of cases, a professional locksmith can open your door within minutes, without any damage. This guide explains exactly what to do when you're locked out, how to avoid the many scams in this sector, and how to choose a reliable professional who won't take advantage of your urgent situation.

Being locked out is stressful, but it shouldn't become a financial disaster. Take the time to read this guide and save the number of a reliable locksmith before you need one – it's the best protection against emergency scams.

First Steps to Take

Before calling a locksmith, take 5 minutes to check these points. You might save yourself an unnecessary expense:

Window left open?

Check if an accessible window was left open, even slightly. On the ground floor or via an accessible balcony, this is sometimes the simplest and free solution. Be careful not to put yourself in danger though – climbing is risky.

Spare key?

A trusted neighbor, family member, or your building manager might have a spare key. Now is the time to check your contact list. Think of all the people you might have given a spare to over the years.

Landlord / Agency?

If you're renting, your landlord or real estate agency often has a spare set of keys. Even in the evening or on weekends, try to reach them. Many agents provide an emergency number for such situations.

Door just pushed?

Sometimes the door isn't really locked. The latch might not be fully engaged. Try gently pushing the door while operating the handle. A firm shoulder push sometimes does the trick if the door wasn't properly shut.

Another access?

Garage door, cellar door, back door, garden access... Some homes have multiple entrances. Check all possibilities before calling a locksmith. Don't forget the garage if it connects to the house.

Concierge / Caretaker?

In buildings with a concierge or caretaker, they sometimes have a master key or can help you find a solution. They may also know if a neighbor has your spare key.

What NOT to Do

In the stress of the moment, some people try solutions that make the situation worse. Here's what you should absolutely avoid:

Never force the lock - You risk damaging the mechanism, turning a simple opening into a complete lock replacement. What could have been a quick intervention becomes a major repair.
Don't use a credit card - This technique only works in movies. Modern locks are designed to resist this type of attempt. You'll damage your card without results and possibly hurt yourself.
Don't break the window - The cost of replacing a window (especially double glazing) far exceeds that of a locksmith. Not to mention the risk of serious injury from broken glass.
Don't try to pick the lock - Without professional training, you risk breaking pins and permanently jamming the lock. You'll make the locksmith's job harder and more expensive.
Don't call the first Google result - Scams are frequent in this sector. Take time to verify the locksmith, even in an emergency. A few minutes of verification can save you significant money.
Don't remove the door from hinges - This rarely works with modern doors and can damage both the door and frame. Leave it to professionals.

Beware of Scams

The emergency locksmith sector is unfortunately plagued by numerous scams. Fraudulent companies take advantage of your stress and urgency to overcharge. Here are the warning signs:

Very low initial quote

"Opening from €29" - These unrealistic prices always hide extras that inflate the final bill: travel, emergency, night, special equipment... The final bill can be several times higher.

No quote beforehand

An honest locksmith always announces their price before starting. Categorically refuse any intervention without an agreed price upfront. "I'll tell you when I see it" is a red flag.

Systematic replacement

Some claim the lock is "broken" and needs complete replacement. In 95% of cases, a simple non-destructive opening is sufficient. If they insist on replacement, get a second opinion.

Cash only payment

A serious professional offers multiple payment methods and provides an invoice with their VAT number. Cash-only demands are a sign of illegal activity.

No identifiable company

A real professional has a verifiable business, a physical address, and a website. Be wary of numbers that only appear in ads without any business presence.

Pressure tactics

Scammers may try to create urgency: "If I don't do it now, it'll be more expensive tomorrow." A professional respects your right to think and compare.

How to Choose a Good Locksmith

Even in an emergency, take a few minutes to check these essential points. It could save you from a costly scam:

Belgian VAT number - A legal locksmith has a business registration number (BCE) verifiable on the FPS Economy website. This is the first guarantee of seriousness. No VAT number = illegal business.
Physical address - Beware of PO boxes or vague addresses. A real craftsman has premises you can verify on Google Maps. A real workshop is a sign of established business.
Price quoted by phone - Demand a firm price BEFORE the locksmith travels. A serious professional can estimate the cost of a standard opening based on your lock type and time of day.
Reasonable night surcharge - A surcharge for night or weekend intervention is normal, but should be proportional and announced upfront. Avoid anyone who won't specify the surcharge in advance.
Verifiable Google reviews - Check online reviews, but beware of profiles with only recent 5-star reviews. Real reviews are varied, detailed, and spread over time. Look for specific details about interventions.
Identified vehicle - A professional locksmith usually arrives in a vehicle marked with their company name. An unmarked personal car can be a warning sign.

How a Professional Opening Works

Understanding the process helps you know what to expect from a legitimate professional:

1. Identity verification - The locksmith will ask for proof that you live at this address (mail, rental contract, ID card with address, etc.). This is a legal requirement to prevent illegal entry.
2. Lock diagnosis - They examine the lock and door to determine the best opening technique. Different locks require different approaches.
3. Price confirmation - Before starting any work, they confirm the price agreed by phone. If there's a difference, they must explain why before proceeding.
4. Non-destructive opening - Depending on the lock type, they use different techniques: picking, bypass, extraction tools... A skilled locksmith rarely needs to drill or damage the lock.
5. Lock verification - Once the door is open, they check that the lock works correctly and hasn't been damaged during the opening.
6. Proper invoice - They provide a detailed invoice with their company name, VAT number, and breakdown of services. This is essential for insurance claims.

Opening Techniques Explained

Professional locksmiths have several non-destructive techniques at their disposal:

Picking

Using specialized tools to manipulate the lock's pins, mimicking the action of the key. This requires skill and works on most standard cylinders without any damage.

Bump key

A specially cut key is inserted and tapped to jostle the pins into position. Quick and effective on many pin tumbler locks, completely non-destructive.

Bypass tools

Specialized instruments that work around the lock mechanism entirely, often used when the latch hasn't fully engaged. Leaves no trace.

Decoder

Advanced tools that read the lock's internal configuration, allowing the locksmith to create a working key or directly manipulate the mechanism.

A professional tries all non-destructive methods before considering drilling. If a locksmith wants to drill immediately without trying other techniques, question their competence.

Insurance Coverage

Good news: your insurance may cover locksmith intervention in certain cases:

Home insurance - Some contracts include "home emergency" assistance that covers locksmith fees for lockouts. Check the "assistance" section of your policy.
Included assistance - Check if you subscribed to a "24h assistance" option with your insurance. Many Belgian insurers offer this as a standard or optional add-on.
Premium bank card - Some Visa/Mastercard Gold or Premium cards include home assistance services, including locksmith coverage.
Keep the invoice - Even if you're not sure you're covered, always keep the invoice. You can submit a reimbursement request later. A proper invoice is essential for any claim.
Call before if possible - If you have time, call your insurer's assistance line before the locksmith. They may send their own approved provider.

Preventing Future Lockouts

Once this mishap is over, take measures to prevent it from happening again:

Make spare keys - Give a spare to a trusted neighbor, family member, or keep one at the office. Multiple locations mean multiple backup options.
Install a knob lock - Some locks only lock with the key from outside, making it impossible to accidentally lock yourself out. Ask your locksmith about this option.
Distinctive keychain - A bulky or noisy keychain will remind you to take your keys. Make it hard to leave without noticing.
Smart lock - Smart locks open with a code or your smartphone, eliminating the need for physical keys. Some even have auto-unlock when your phone approaches.
Key routine - Develop a habit of always putting keys in the same place and checking before closing the door. Routine prevents forgetfulness.
Save a reliable number - Keep a trusted locksmith's number in your phone now. In an emergency, you'll be glad you already know who to call.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a locked door be opened without breaking the lock?

Yes, in 95% of cases a professional locksmith opens the door without any damage using non-destructive techniques like picking, bypass, or specialized tools. Only very high-security locks or damaged mechanisms may require replacement. If a locksmith claims they must drill immediately, get a second opinion.

How long does it take to open a locked door?

The opening itself typically takes between 5 and 15 minutes for a standard lock. The locksmith's arrival time is approximately 30 minutes in major Belgian cities. In total, expect 45 minutes to 1 hour between your call and door opening, depending on traffic and location.

Does my insurance cover the intervention?

Some home insurance policies include locksmith assistance. Check your contract or call your insurer before calling a locksmith if you have time. Even if you're not covered, always keep the detailed invoice – some insurers reimburse upon presentation of proper receipts.

Can the locksmith intervene at night or on weekends?

Yes, emergency locksmiths like Janssens operate 24/7, including nights, weekends, and holidays. A surcharge for out-of-hours intervention is normal but must be announced in advance and remain reasonable. Always confirm the total price before agreeing to service.

How do I prove I live at this address?

The locksmith is legally required to verify your identity before opening a door. You can present: your ID card with the address, a rental contract, an energy bill, recent mail in your name, or ask a neighbor to confirm your identity. This protects both you and the locksmith legally.

What if I don't have my ID on me?

If you have no documents, the locksmith can ask a neighbor to confirm your identity, or you can call someone who will come with your papers. As a last resort, the police can intervene to confirm your residence. It may take longer, but there are always solutions.

Will the lock be damaged after opening?

No, with modern fine opening techniques, your lock remains perfectly functional after the intervention. You can continue to use your keys normally. If a locksmith tells you otherwise without trying non-destructive methods first, be suspicious – it may be a scam to sell you a new lock.

Do I need to change my lock after an opening?

No, it's not necessary after a simple lockout. Your lock wasn't forced and remains secure. Changing is only recommended in case of burglary, lost keys, or if the lock was already worn or damaged before the lockout.
